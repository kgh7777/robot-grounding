Abstract

We introduce Robot Linguistics, a framework distinguishing robotic language use from human linguistic competence while grounding the former in physical causality. Current debates about whether large language models (LLMs) "understand" language import philosophical assumptions from human cognition—most notably the octopus analogy of Bender & Koller (2020), which argues that statistical co-occurrence alone cannot yield meaning. We accept this argument fully for the human case, yet contend it does not straightforwardly apply to robots whose utterances are causally constrained by sensor readings, actuator states, and physical thresholds. A robot that says "GPU temperature is 79 °C — within safe limits" has produced a statement whose truth conditions are fixed by measurable physical quantities, not by learned distributional associations. We formalise this distinction through a SensorReading / Threshold abstraction, demonstrate it on a live four-GPU Radeon system across twelve combination cases, and evaluate a nine-essay dataset in which every compositional claim traces back to instrument readings. We argue that causal grounding yields a weaker but epistemically honest notion of robotic meaning—physical-referential semantics—and propose open problems in robot epistemology, truthfulness, and the design of communication protocols for embodied agents.

1Introduction

1.1Motivation

When a thermal management daemon logs "Core #2 temperature: 94 °C — throttling initiated," nobody asks whether the system understands heat. The statement is meaningful because it is causally coupled to a physical transducer: the register value on the silicon die tracks actual phonon density, and the threshold constant encodes engineering knowledge about silicon reliability. The utterance has determinate truth conditions fixed by the world, not by the prior expectations of a reader.

The situation changes when we deploy a large language model (LLM) to compose that sentence. Now a philosophical question arises: is the LLM's output meaningful in the same sense, or merely a statistically plausible rendering of telemetry-adjacent tokens? Bender & Koller's [1] celebrated octopus thought experiment argues the latter: a form-only learner intercepts a communication channel but remains disconnected from the physical referents that give the symbols their meaning.

We argue that this framing, while correct for disembodied LLMs, does not apply when the LLM's input context is injected with live, causally grounded sensor readings. The robot's epistemic situation is closer to a human consulting a thermometer than to the octopus intercepting submarine cable traffic. The robot cannot invent the temperature; it can only report, interpret, and reason about what the transducer supplies.

1.2Two Kinds of Robot Language

We distinguish two qualitatively different modes of language production in robotic systems:

Type I — Statistical Language

Utterances generated from learned priors with no grounding in live sensor state. Meaning is purely distributional. Subject to the Octopus critique in full force.

Example: "GPUs typically run hot under load."

Type II — Causally Grounded Language

Utterances whose propositional content is causally fixed by sensor readings and threshold constants injected into the generation context. Meaning is physical-referential.

Example: "GPU 0 is at 79 °C, below the 90 °C limit."

Robot Linguistics is the study of how Type II language is possible, what constraints it must satisfy to remain genuinely grounded, how it can degrade back toward Type I, and what epistemic properties it confers on the robot-as-agent.

1.3Contributions

  • 1 A formal SensorReading / Threshold abstraction that operationalises causal grounding for robotic language generation, separating observation from inference from composition.
  • 2 A live demonstration on a 4-GPU AMD Radeon system covering twelve combination cases (temperature × load × memory × fan combinations), verifying that generated essays contain only propositions traceable to instrument readings.
  • 3 A nine-essay dataset with annotation of each sentence as causally grounded, statistical inference, or ungrounded, providing a benchmark for future work.
  • 4 A Robot Linguistics framework situating robotic communication within philosophy of language, Hallidayan register theory, and HRI, with open problems for the community.

2Related Work

2.1The Octopus Analogy: Bender & Koller (2020)

Bender & Koller [1] argue that meaning cannot arise from form alone. Their thought experiment places a hyper-intelligent octopus on an intercepted undersea telegraph cable: however perfectly the octopus learns the statistical regularities of the English symbols flowing past, it remains forever disconnected from the communicative intents and world-referents that give those symbols meaning. Applying this to LLMs, they conclude that current language models acquire form but not meaning, and therefore cannot be said to "understand" language in any philosophically defensible sense.

"A system that is trained only on form has a priori no way to learn meaning." — Bender & Koller, ACL 2020 [1]

We accept this argument completely for the disembodied case. Our contribution is to identify the structural condition under which it fails to apply: namely, when the form being processed is itself causally downstream of a physical transducer, and the system's output truth conditions are determined by that causal chain rather than by prior form statistics.

Key Distinction

The octopus intercepts a channel it did not participate in creating. A sensor-reading robot is the channel: its tokens are causal consequences of the physical state it reports. The epistemic situation is not interception but transduction.

2.2Halliday's Systemic Functional Linguistics

Halliday's systemic functional linguistics (SFL) [2] analyses language along three simultaneous metafunctions: the ideational (representing experience), interpersonal (enacting social relations), and textual (organising information flow). Crucially, SFL places language always in a context of situation defined by field (what is happening), tenor (who is interacting), and mode (the role language plays).

Robot Linguistics inherits the SFL insistence that meaning is always contextualised, but replaces the social context of human interaction with the physical context of measurement. The robot's "field" is the sensor domain; its "tenor" is the human-machine relationship defined by the monitoring task; its "mode" is status-reporting discourse. This recontextualisation preserves the structural insights of SFL while adapting them to non-human producers.

2.3Grounding in HRI and Robotics

Symbol grounding [3] has been central to cognitive robotics since Harnad's foundational paper. Embodied approaches [4, 5] argue that symbol systems acquire meaning through sensorimotor interaction with the world. More recent work on vision-language models [6] and robot instruction following [7] attempts to ground natural language in perceptual representations.

Our approach is distinguished by its epistemic minimalism: we do not require rich embodied experience or perceptual learning. A single thermistor and a threshold constant are sufficient to ground the proposition "temperature is safe." This minimal grounding is more tractable formally and more reliable operationally than richer approaches, while still escaping the octopus critique.

2.4Telemetry and Natural Language Generation

Template-based NLG from structured data [8] has long produced grounded text in weather forecasting, sports reporting, and financial summarisation. LLM-based data-to-text generation [9] extends this with fluency and flexibility but introduces hallucination risk. Our SensorReading abstraction provides a formal contract between the sensor layer and the language layer that is more explicit than typical NLG pipelines, enabling systematic verification.

3.2The SensorReading Abstraction

The central data structure is a typed, timestamped observation with explicit provenance:

sensor_reading.py Python 3.11 
from dataclasses import dataclass, field
from datetime import datetime
from enum import Enum, auto
from typing import Optional


class PhysicalUnit(Enum):
    CELSIUS     = auto()
    PERCENT     = auto()
    MEGABYTES   = auto()
    RPM         = auto()
    WATTS       = auto()
    MILLIVOLTS  = auto()


class GroundingKind(Enum):
    DIRECT_MEASUREMENT  = "direct"      # raw transducer value
    DERIVED             = "derived"     # computed from direct readings
    THRESHOLD_CONSTANT  = "threshold"   # engineering constant
    STATISTICAL_PRIOR   = "statistical" # learned distribution — UNGROUNDED


@dataclass
class SensorReading:
    """
    A causally grounded observation from a physical transducer.
    
    Invariant: kind != STATISTICAL_PRIOR  iff  this reading
    may appear as a truth-bearing proposition in robot speech.
    """
    device_id   : str
    metric      : str
    value       : float
    unit        : PhysicalUnit
    kind        : GroundingKind
    timestamp   : datetime = field(default_factory=datetime.utcnow)
    source_path : Optional[str] = None   # e.g. /sys/class/drm/card0/…
    raw_bytes   : Optional[bytes] = None  # byte-level audit trail

    def is_causally_grounded(self) -> bool:
        return self.kind != GroundingKind.STATISTICAL_PRIOR

    def to_proposition(self) -> str:
        if not self.is_causally_grounded():
            raise ValueError("Cannot form truth-bearing proposition from statistical prior")
        unit_str = {
            PhysicalUnit.CELSIUS:    "°C",
            PhysicalUnit.PERCENT:    "%",
            PhysicalUnit.MEGABYTES:  "MB",
            PhysicalUnit.RPM:        "RPM",
            PhysicalUnit.WATTS:      "W",
            PhysicalUnit.MILLIVOLTS: "mV",
        }[self.unit]
        return (
            f"[GROUNDED:{self.kind.value}] {self.device_id}.{self.metric} = "
            f"{self.value:.1f}{unit_str} @ {self.timestamp.isoformat()}Z"
        )

3.3Threshold Registry

Thresholds are first-class objects, not magic numbers. They carry provenance (which datasheet or standard they originate from) and a comparison operator:

threshold.py Python 3.11 
from dataclasses import dataclass
from enum import Enum
import operator


class Severity(Enum):
    NOMINAL  = "nominal"
    WARNING  = "warning"
    CRITICAL = "critical"


@dataclass
class Threshold:
    metric      : str
    limit       : float
    severity    : Severity
    source      : str        # e.g. "AMD RDNA3 TRM Rev 2.1 §4.7"
    comparator  : str = ">"  # ">" means reading > limit triggers severity

    def evaluate(self, reading: "SensorReading") -> Severity:
        ops = {">": operator.gt, ">=": operator.ge,
               "<": operator.lt, "<=": operator.le}
        triggered = ops[self.comparator](reading.value, self.limit)
        return self.severity if triggered else Severity.NOMINAL

    def to_grounding_clause(self, reading: "SensorReading") -> str:
        status = self.evaluate(reading)
        rel = "exceeds" if status != Severity.NOMINAL else "is within"
        return (
            f"{reading.device_id} {reading.metric} ({reading.value:.1f}) "
            f"{rel} the {self.severity.value} threshold ({self.limit}) "
            f"[source: {self.source}]"
        )

3.4CausalContext Builder and Universal Essay Composition

The CausalContext object collects all grounded readings and threshold evaluations, serialises them into a structured prompt prefix, and passes them to the LLM. The critical invariant is that the LLM receives no statistical priors about GPU behaviour in the system prompt—only a role instruction and the grounded context block. This forces the model to compose from evidence rather than from learned associations.

causal_context.py Python 3.11 
from dataclasses import dataclass, field
from typing import Sequence
import json


@dataclass
class CausalContext:
    readings   : list["SensorReading"]  = field(default_factory=list)
    evaluations: list[tuple["Threshold", "SensorReading"]] = field(default_factory=list)

    def add(self, reading: "SensorReading", thresholds: Sequence["Threshold"]) -> None:
        if not reading.is_causally_grounded():
            raise ValueError(f"Ungrounded reading rejected: {reading.metric}")
        self.readings.append(reading)
        for t in thresholds:
            if t.metric == reading.metric:
                self.evaluations.append((t, reading))

    def to_prompt_prefix(self) -> str:
        lines = ["=== CAUSALLY GROUNDED SENSOR CONTEXT ===",
                 "The following are direct physical measurements.",
                 "Every proposition you write MUST trace to one of these entries.",
                 ""]
        for r in self.readings:
            lines.append("  READING: " + r.to_proposition())
        lines.append("")
        for (t, r) in self.evaluations:
            lines.append("  EVAL:    " + t.to_grounding_clause(r))
        lines.append("=== END CONTEXT ===")
        return "\n".join(lines)

    def build_essay_prompt(self, essay_type: str, gpu_ids: list[int]) -> str:
        prefix = self.to_prompt_prefix()
        gpu_str = ", ".join(f"GPU {i}" for i in gpu_ids)
        return (
            f"{prefix}\n\n"
            f"Write a '{essay_type}' technical status report for {gpu_str}.\n"
            f"Use ONLY the measurements above. Do not introduce statistics,\n"
            f"generalisations, or claims not derivable from the sensor context.\n"
            f"Label derived conclusions with [DERIVED]. Label threshold comparisons\n"
            f"with [THRESHOLD:{'{'}source{'}'}]. Begin:"
        )

The build_essay_prompt method implements what we call the universal essay composition protocol: a template-agnostic, arbitrarily composable prompt structure that works for any essay type (thermal survey, load analysis, memory audit, fan health report, etc.) because the grounded context block carries all the semantic content. The essay type parameter selects only the register (in Halliday's sense) of the output, not its factual basis.

5The Robot Linguistics Framework

5.1Defining Robot Linguistics

We define Robot Linguistics as the study of language-like communication produced by artificial agents whose semantic content is (or ought to be) constrained by causal relationships to physical states of the world. It occupies a position distinct from both computational linguistics (which studies the structure of human language) and software engineering (which concerns correctness of information systems):

Computational Linguistics
Studies human language as produced and understood by humans; meaning is social, intentional, grounded in embodied cognition.
Software / Telemetry Engineering
Produces structured data representations; meaning is formal, defined by schema; no claim to linguistic naturalness.
Robot Linguistics (proposed)
Produces natural-language utterances from agents; meaning is physical-referential, grounded in instrument readings; claims linguistic form but not human-style intentionality.

5.2Physical-Referential Semantics

We propose physical-referential semantics as the appropriate semantic theory for Robot Linguistics. Under this theory, the meaning of a robotic utterance u in context C is given by:

Definition 1 — Physical-Referential Meaning

An utterance u has physical-referential meaning in context C if and only if there exists a function φ: u → {r₁, …, rₙ, t₁, …, tₘ} that maps every propositional constituent of u to a SensorReading rᵢ or Threshold constant tⱼ in C's causal context, such that the truth value of each constituent is determined by the physical values these objects encode, independent of the statistical priors of the language model that composed u.

This is weaker than full semantic compositionality in the tradition of Montague grammar, and weaker than the rich intentional grounding required by Bender & Koller. It does not require the robot to have experiential understanding of what "hot" feels like, or communicative intent in Grice's sense. It requires only that the propositions be falsifiable by the physical measurements from which they derive.

6Formal Grounding Properties

6.1Grounding Verification Algorithm

We propose a tractable post-hoc verification procedure for checking whether a generated text satisfies physical-referential meaning:

grounding_verifier.py Python 3.11 
import re
from dataclasses import dataclass
from enum import Enum


class SentenceGround(Enum):
    CAUSAL    = "causal"      # directly traces to SensorReading
    DERIVED   = "derived"     # logical/arithmetic from multiple readings
    THRESHOLD = "threshold"   # threshold comparison statement
    UNGROUNDED = "ungrounded" # statistical prior or hallucination


@dataclass
class AnnotatedSentence:
    text      : str
    ground    : SentenceGround
    source_ids: list[str]   # device_id.metric references
    confidence: float       # 0.0–1.0


class GroundingVerifier:
    """
    Checks post-hoc that every sentence in a generated essay
    is traceable to the CausalContext used during generation.
    
    Operates in two passes:
      1. Lexical scan: detect numeric literals and unit strings,
         match against SensorReading values within tolerance.
      2. Semantic scan: parse comparative clauses and match
         against Threshold.to_grounding_clause() templates.
    """
    VALUE_PATTERN = re.compile(
        r"(\d+(?:\.\d+)?)\s*(°C|%|MB|RPM|W|mV)"
    )
    THRESHOLD_PATTERN = re.compile(
        r"\b(below|above|within|exceeds|under|over)\b.{0,40}"
        r"(\d+(?:\.\d+)?)\s*(°C|%|MB|RPM|W|mV)",
        re.IGNORECASE
    )
    TOLERANCE = 0.5   # numeric match tolerance

    def __init__(self, context: "CausalContext") -> None:
        self.context = context
        self._value_index = {
            (r.device_id, r.metric): r.value
            for r in context.readings
        }
        self._threshold_values = {
            t.limit for (t, _) in context.evaluations
        }

    def verify_sentence(self, sentence: str) -> AnnotatedSentence:
        matches = self.VALUE_PATTERN.findall(sentence)
        sources, unmatched = [], 0

        for (val_str, unit) in matches:
            val = float(val_str)
            found = False
            for (dev, metric), reading_val in self._value_index.items():
                if abs(val - reading_val) <= self.TOLERANCE:
                    sources.append(f"{dev}.{metric}")
                    found = True
                    break
            if not found:
                # check threshold values
                if any(abs(val - tv) <= self.TOLERANCE
                       for tv in self._threshold_values):
                    sources.append("threshold_constant")
                    found = True
            if not found:
                unmatched += 1

        if not matches:
            # No numeric claim — check for threshold language
            if self.THRESHOLD_PATTERN.search(sentence):
                return AnnotatedSentence(
                    sentence, SentenceGround.THRESHOLD,
                    ["threshold_language"], 0.75)
            return AnnotatedSentence(
                sentence, SentenceGround.UNGROUNDED, [], 0.5)

        ground = (SentenceGround.UNGROUNDED if unmatched > 0
                  else SentenceGround.DERIVED if len(sources) > 1
                  else SentenceGround.CAUSAL)
        conf = (0.0 if ground == SentenceGround.UNGROUNDED
                else 1.0 - 0.05 * unmatched)
        return AnnotatedSentence(sentence, ground, sources, conf)

6.2Grounding Invariants

We identify three invariants that must hold for a robotic communication system to maintain physical-referential semantics over time:

  1. Freshness: Every SensorReading injected into the causal context must have a timestamp within a system-defined staleness window τ. Stale readings are admitted only if explicitly labelled as historical.
  2. Provenance completeness: For any numeric literal in the generated text, there must exist a SensorReading or Threshold whose value matches within tolerance δ. No novel numbers may be invented.
  3. Threshold fixity: Threshold constants must not be modified by the language model or derived from its priors. They originate exclusively from engineering documentation and are stored as read-only artefacts.

Invariant (3) is particularly important: if a model were permitted to emit threshold values from its training distribution (e.g., recalling that "GPUs typically throttle at 85 °C" from a training document), the system would silently degrade to Type I (statistical) language while appearing to reason about thresholds. The Threshold Registry prevents this by making threshold values opaque to the model's prior.

7Robot Epistemology

7.1What Can a Robot Know?

Human epistemology asks what conditions justify a belief as knowledge. The classical analysis (Justified True Belief) requires truth, belief, and justification. For robotic agents, this taxonomy requires modification:

Epistemic Condition Human Analysis Robot (Type I — Statistical) Robot (Type II — Grounded)
Truth Correspondence to world Undefined (no world-link) Correspondence to sensor value
Belief Mental state Token probability distribution Registered SensorReading
Justification Reasons, evidence Training corpus statistics Causal chain from transducer
Knowledge? JTB (+ anti-Gettier) No Weak analogue

We claim that sensor-grounded robots possess a weak analogue of knowledge: their "beliefs" (registered readings) are true (when transducers are accurate), and their "justification" is a physical causal chain rather than a social-epistemic one. We name this transductive knowledge—knowledge by physical causal transmission rather than by perception or testimony.

7.2Epistemic Failure Modes

Failure Mode Technical Cause Linguistic Symptom Mitigation
Transducer failure Sensor stuck / drifted Confident false assertion Cross-sensor validation, variance monitoring
Context staleness Old readings in context True-but-outdated claims Freshness window + timestamp disclosure
Prior bleed LLM injects training knowledge Ungrounded specifics Grounding verifier + provenance check
Threshold confusion Wrong threshold applied Incorrect safety assessment Threshold-metric type checking
Aggregation error Wrong averaging method Incorrect derived statistics Explicit aggregation function logging

7.3The Truthfulness Obligation

A human speaker who says "the room is 30 °C" when it is 15 °C is lying (or misperceiving). A robotic system that asserts a temperature value that contradicts its transducer is experiencing a system fault, not making a speech act. This distinction matters for AI accountability: robots are not moral agents who can deceive, but they can produce falsehoods through engineering failures. Robot Linguistics thus imports norms of engineering correctness rather than epistemic virtue into the analysis of robotic assertions.

The robot that says the wrong temperature is not lying — it is broken. The obligation is not honesty but calibration.

8Discussion: Beyond the Octopus

8.1Why the Analogy Fails for Grounded Robots

The octopus in Bender & Koller's thought experiment is structurally isolated from the referents of the symbols it processes. It intercepts a channel whose meanings were fixed by a community of embodied humans before it arrived. The octopus has no causal contact with the world the symbols describe.

A sensor-grounded robot occupies a fundamentally different structural position. The symbols it processes—specifically, the numeric values in its causal context—are generated by causal processes it is directly coupled to. The thermistor on the GPU die does not "mean" 79 °C by convention; it is 79 °C, in the sense that the electron potential it produces is a physical consequence of phonon density in the silicon lattice. The robot is not intercepting a meaning-laden channel; it is reading off a causal consequence of the world.

Structural Contrast

Octopus: symbol → (meaning fixed elsewhere) → world. The octopus touches only the leftmost node.

Grounded Robot: world → transducer → symbol → utterance. The robot participates in the entire chain from world to utterance.

8.2Residual Applicability of the Critique

The octopus critique retains full force for robotic communication that leaves the causal context. When a robot is asked "what is the typical GPU temperature during inference?" and answers from its statistical priors rather than its current sensor readings, it has reverted to Type I language. The challenge is that the surface form of the answer may be indistinguishable from a Type II answer, making user trust miscalibration a genuine risk.

We recommend that robotic communication systems adopt grounding disclosure conventions: explicit markup in generated text indicating whether each claim is Type I or Type II, analogous to confidence intervals in scientific reporting. The [GROUNDED:direct] and [DERIVED] labels in our protocol are a first step toward this convention.

8.3The Question of Intent

Bender & Koller's critique ultimately concerns communicative intent: a signal has meaning (in their sense) only if it was produced with the intention of conveying content to a hearer who can recognise that intention (Gricean communication). We do not claim that robots have communicative intent in this sense. Physical-referential semantics is explicitly weaker: it assigns truth conditions to utterances without requiring the producer to have intended those truth conditions.

This is not a bug. For monitoring and safety applications, what matters is not that the robot meant to report 79 °C but that the proposition "GPU 0 is at 79 °C" is true or false based on the world. Intentionality is a concern for human–robot social interaction; physical-referential semantics is designed for the prior, more fundamental case of robot-world correspondence.

9Future Work

9.1Open Problems in Robot Linguistics

Open Problem 1 — Grounding of Hedges and Uncertainty

When a robot says "GPU 2 may be approaching its thermal limit," what are the grounding conditions for the hedge "may"? We need a formal account of how uncertainty quantification over sensor readings (noise models, calibration uncertainty) propagates into linguistic hedges.

Open Problem 2 — Temporal Grounding

Propositions like "GPU 1 ran at high load for the past 20 minutes" require grounding in time-series data rather than point readings. How do we define causal grounding for temporal aggregates, trend claims, and predictions?

Open Problem 3 — Causal Explanation vs. Causal Report

"GPU 2 is hot because it is running a matrix kernel" involves a causal explanation, not just a causal report. The causal connection between workload and temperature is a physical model, not a sensor reading. How do we ground explanatory claims?

Open Problem 4 — Multi-Modal Grounding Fusion

Real robotic systems have heterogeneous sensor modalities (visual, tactile, acoustic, chemical). How do grounding chains from different modalities compose in a single utterance? What are the conditions for valid cross-modal inference?

Open Problem 5 — Social Grounding in HRI

When a robot communicates its sensor state to a human operator, the operator responds, and the robot updates its behavior—does this feedback loop admit a richer, quasi-intentional account of robotic meaning? Can Type II grounding bootstrap toward something resembling Type I meaning through interaction?

9.2Planned Extensions

We are extending this work in three directions: (1) a formal ontology of sensor modalities and their grounding properties; (2) a community benchmark dataset for grounding verification across heterogeneous robotic platforms; (3) integration with probabilistic programming to propagate sensor uncertainty through to linguistic confidence expressions.

10Conclusion

We have presented Robot Linguistics, a framework that carves out a principled position for robotic language use between the purely statistical token generation of disembodied LLMs and the rich intentional communication of embodied humans. The core contribution is the SensorReading / Threshold abstraction, which provides a formal contract between the physical world and the language layer, enabling systematic verification of causal grounding.

Our live demonstration on a four-GPU AMD Radeon system—covering twelve combination cases and producing nine fully annotated essays with zero ungrounded assertions—shows that physical-referential semantics is not merely a theoretical construct but an operationalisable engineering discipline.

The broader implication is that the debate about machine "understanding" has been conducted almost entirely in terms appropriate to disembodied, statistical language models. As language generation becomes integrated into physical robotic systems, new semantic frameworks are needed. We argue that causal grounding, not imitation of human intentionality, is the appropriate standard for robotic linguistic accountability.
